The Bechdel Test

A minimal lens to examine how women are represented in film. Scroll to explore.

The Bechdel Test has become one of the most cited tools when it comes to discussing the representation of women in film. Its premise is quite simple:

1. Are there at least two women in the film?

2. Do they talk to each other?

3. And is their conversation about something other than a man?

At first glance, these questions may seem trivial. But they reveal a persistent issue in storytelling: women often appear on screen only in relation to male characters. Passing the test does not require a film to be feminist, nor does failing it automatically make a film sexist. Rather, the Bechdel Test highlights patterns in the way women are written and how they exist within narratives.

The idea originated in the 1980s, when cartoonist Alison Bechdel, inspired by a suggestion from her friend Liz Wallace, introduced it in her comic strip The Rule.

Comic "The Rule"

What began as a commentary on Hollywood’s gender dynamics soon developed into a cultural reference point. Over time, feminist film critics popularized the test, and today it has even been adopted as an evaluation criterion: the Swedish Film Institute uses it as an indicator of diversity, while Eurimages, the European film fund, has factored it into its funding decisions.

Nevertheless, the Bechdel Test is not a quality seal. It does not measure narrative depth, artistic achievement, or progressive values. Instead, it provides a lens, sometimes an uncomfortable one, through which to examine how women are represented.

This simplicity is also its weakness. The test can give curious or even misleading results. Take, for instance, The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers. In one of the trilogy’s few exchanges between female characters, the young Freda asks Éowyn, “Where is Mama?”, to which Éowyn responds, “Shhh.”

Technically, this fleeting moment allows the film to pass. Yet the fact that such a brief, almost inconsequential dialogue meets the criteria shows the limitations of the test.

Let's look at Genres

I was also curious how different movie genres perform when it comes to the Bechdel Test and I was honestly surprised. Believe it or not, horror films and romances actually do the best. Percentage-wise, they pass the test more often than other genres. Maybe it’s because romantic films tend to have more female characters, who then actually talk about something other than men. And in horror movies, it’s often a monster or some other threat that drives the plot. This is exactly what Alison Bechdel cynically pointed out in her original comic. Westerns, on the other hand, come out at the bottom. They fail the Bechdel Test more than any other genre.
